Claude can be wrong with complete confidence. That's not a flaw unique to AI — it's a property of any system that produces fluent, articulate output. The problem is that fluency reads as competence. A well-structured paragraph about formation pressure gradients *sounds* authoritative whether or not the underlying reasoning is sound.

In E&P work, the cost of a confident wrong answer can be significant. A miscalculated EMW on a tight-pore-pressure well. A completion design recommendation that doesn't match your actual offset data. A production forecast that bakes in assumptions you never agreed to. You need to know not just what Claude concluded, but how it got there — and where it's uncertain.

The good news: you can ask for that directly.

## The default behavior you need to override

Left to its own devices, Claude optimizes for a helpful, direct answer. That's usually what you want. But in technical work, directness without hedging can mask real uncertainty. Claude may be interpolating from general petroleum engineering principles rather than your specific data. It may be filling in gaps with assumptions it never surfaced. The answer looks complete even when the foundation is thin.

You can override this by building verification prompts into how you work. The goal isn't to make Claude more tentative in general — it's to make it explicit about *where* it's confident and *where* it's guessing.

## Prompting for uncertainty

The simplest intervention: ask Claude to flag its assumptions. When you ask a technical question, append something like: *As you answer, flag any assumptions you're making about formation properties, costs, or conditions that aren't in the documents I've provided. If you're uncertain about something, say so.*

That instruction shifts Claude's behavior meaningfully. Instead of presenting a clean answer, it will annotate the places where it filled in gaps. You'll see things like: *I'm assuming your fluid system is slickwater-based since the completion report doesn't specify a gel loading* — and that assumption might be wrong in ways that change the answer.

Similarly, you can ask Claude to rate its confidence on different parts of an answer, or to separate what the data directly supports from what it's inferring. "Based on the documents, X is clearly the case. The following is my interpretation and may be worth verifying with your completions team" is a far more useful response than a uniform-confidence paragraph.

## Asking for reasoning, not just conclusions

For any technically consequential answer, ask Claude to walk through its reasoning before it states a conclusion. "Explain how you arrived at that" surfaces the chain of logic — and breaks in that chain are usually visible when you can see each step.

This matters especially on quantitative questions. If you're asking Claude to help you think through whether a recompletion is economic at current strip prices, you want to see the inputs it's using, the cost assumptions it's making, and which variables are driving the result. An answer that says "the recompletion looks marginal" is hard to evaluate. An answer that says "at $65 WTI, the workover break-even requires recovering X MBoe incremental, which implies the recompletion interval needs to be contributing at least Y Boe/d above current base decline — here's how I got there" is checkable.

You can run those numbers yourself. You can replace Claude's cost assumptions with your actual vendor quote. You can decide whether the implied recovery is realistic for your zone. The reasoning is the product, not just the conclusion.

## Verification as professional habit

None of this is unique to AI. The same discipline applies to any technical analysis you receive from a junior engineer, a consultant, or a software tool. You'd check a decline curve model by asking what type-curve the analyst used. You'd verify a well cost estimate by walking through the AFE line by line. The instinct to check the work behind the answer is standard engineering practice.

The difference with Claude is that the answers arrive fast and sound polished, which can lull you into skipping the verification step. The fluency is the hazard. Building in the habit of asking "show me your reasoning and flag your assumptions" is just applying the same rigor you'd apply to any other source — before you make a decision that costs money to undo.

## A simple practice

At the end of any technically significant Claude answer, ask one follow-up question: *What would change your answer, and what am I most at risk of getting wrong if I act on this?*

That question forces Claude to surface the sensitive assumptions, the places where your data might not match its model, and the conditions under which its recommendation breaks down. It's the engineering equivalent of a pre-job safety review — fast, cheap, and worth doing every time.
